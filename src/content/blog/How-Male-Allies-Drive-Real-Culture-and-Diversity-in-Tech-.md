Let's start with a simple exercise. Try naming 10 women currently in prominent leadership roles in the global tech industry without using a search engine. When I tried this myself, I struggled. Truth be told, the first names that come to mind are retrospective. Even now, I can see a couple of faces in my head, but do I remember their names? Honestly, no. Where's the progress in that?

Now, how many men can you name in 5 seconds? I'm rattling the names off in my head with such ease, it's almost uncomfortable. And nothing short of a stark reminder that the tech industry needs men, women and non-binary people to come together and support each other as allies to keep each other in the industry side by side.

In a world where Diversity, Equity and Inclusion (DEI) is being disbanded as quickly as it came into the spotlight as a matter of importance, and whilst mostly men still lead the industry, it's clear there's much work to be done. The tech sector is facing a massive self-inflicted talent shortage. Progression involves having male allies who are willing to play a critical role in addressing gender imbalance by using their majority position to actively support, advocate for, and amplify underrepresented voices, more than ever.

If we want to solve this crisis, it's time to look at how young women view a future in technology because when more than half of our prospective talent pool is actively dismissing our industry, we are operating at a massive competitive disadvantage. The current outlook is a stark wake-up call.

## Did you know…?

* Only [27%](https://www.business-reporter.co.uk/human-resources/gender-equality-in-the-male-dominated-tech-industry) of female students would consider a career in technology, compared with 61% of their male counterparts. 
* Even more critical, only [3%](https://www.pwc.co.uk/who-we-are/leadership-and-people/inclusion/her-tech-talent/time-to-close-the-gender-gap.html) of females say a career in technology is their first choice.
* Only [26%](https://www.womenintech.co.uk/8-facts-women-tech-industry/) of the STEM workforce in the UK and US is women.
* The bottleneck tightens at the top: just [5%](https://www.pwc.co.uk/who-we-are/leadership-and-people/inclusion/her-tech-talent/time-to-close-the-gender-gap.html) of leadership roles across the tech industry are held by women. 

The most shocking and scariest figure:

* It will take [over 130 years](https://www.grantthornton.global/en/insights/articles/the-role-of-male-allies-in-progressing-towards-gender-parity/) to close the global gender gap. 

The irony is that there isn't the time to wait over a century for the industry to "naturally" balance itself. We need the 95% of current leaders - **the men in the room** - to treat this as a high-priority business objective. Yet this is layered; it goes much deeper and takes emotional intelligence too. In the words of Benjamin Ventham, an HR Technology leader at AstraZeneca, ["it's fundamentally about doing the right thing; striving for equality"](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/women-tech-importance-men-allies-ben-ventham/). Using male privilege for good. To reach gender parity at a faster pace, it makes sense for men and women to join forces, since men are currently in a better position to bring about change and are therefore an essential part of the solution. 

## Allyship as an Operational Competency 

Seeing as men hold the vast majority of decision-making power, active allyship is a **core leadership competency**.

Moving the needle requires male executives to treat allyship as they would any other high-stakes project: by setting clear expectations, advocating for top talent behind closed doors, and proactively elevating underrepresented voices. This is about driving organisational excellence from the top down.

### What does this look like in daily execution? 

* It can be as simple as **challenging biases in the moment**: be the one who does not tolerate toxic behaviours, microaggressions, or "locker room" humour - call it out. Publicly defend anyone who male colleagues have sexually harassed. Stepping in to stop harassment or bias isn't just about chivalry; it's about setting a new normal and protecting the integrity of your team's operating environment.
* **Amplify voices**: ensure clear attribution of ideas. Research by Creary, Caza, and Gibson highlights that truly effective allyship by male leaders means actively ensuring that contributions from all team members receive appropriate recognition and are put to use, helping to prevent the loss of innovation. When a woman's idea is ignored in a meeting, overlooked, or later co-opted by a colleague, the company loses out on its creative assets.
* **Implementing Structural Optimisations**: Creary and her co-authors also note that authentic structural advocacy involves men using their influence to improve organisational design and culture in ways that support inclusion. This means treating initiatives like equal pay transparency, blind recruitment, flexible working hours, and the normalisation of parental leave across all genders as strategic business tools. These are not corporate favours; they are necessary system updates designed to remove bias from hiring and create a highly agile, sustainable workforce.
* Leadership excellence requires taking direct ownership of the culture. **A true changemaker** does not delegate cultural health to an HR committee or an external consultant. They assume personal accountability, publicly defending performance over prejudice and actively dismantling stereotypes. Waiting for others to act is a failure of initiative; setting a high standard for psychological safety and equitable growth is what it means to command a room.

## The Cost of the "Leaky Bucket" 

The leaky bucket phenomenon refers to women leaving the industry mid-career. This attrition is driven by a combination of factors:  pervasive 'bro culture', being passed over for early promotions, or promotions at all, to the lack of sponsorship or mentorship, and inadequate flexibility and support during key life stages.

What is the direct business impact of an operational shift? It eliminates a massive source of organisational friction.

When women and underrepresented groups are forced to constantly self-advocate, navigate microaggressions, or push back against toxic mindsets, they are wasting valuable mental bandwidth. That is energy stolen from innovation, coding, and driving product value. By alleviating the expectation that minority groups defend themselves, male allies help reduce the cultural barriers that can hinder high performance.

In traditionally male-dominated sectors such as technology, leaders who identify and support underrepresented talent distinguish organisations that adapt and progress from those that remain stagnant [(Guzmán et al, 2023)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10664-023-10379-8). Removing this friction creates a high-velocity culture where teams can focus entirely on executing their best work, driving a systemic transformation across the entire company.

The ultimate proof of our leadership deficit lies in our retention data. According to the [2025 Lovelace Report](https://wearetechwomen.com/2025-lovelace-report/), the UK tech sector loses between £2 billion and £3.5 billion each year due to women leaving the industry, highlighting the significant economic impact of female attrition in enterprise technology. 

From a business standpoint, this is a catastrophic haemorrhage of intellectual capital and training ROI. We are spending a decade developing top-tier engineering and leadership talent, only to watch them walk out the door at the exact moment they reach peak professional value.

To address this retention crisis, [research by Brandy L. Simula](https://hbr.org/2023/05/we-need-men-to-be-better-allies-sooner) notes that updating company policies to include flexible working hours and equitable parental leave is more than just offering workplace perks; it is a crucial approach to retaining talent. When men take action to support these kinds of changes, they help protect company investments, combat mid-career attrition, and create clearer advancement opportunities for high-performing women, especially as many women continue to face workplace biases and barriers to career growth, which flatten their trajectories for long-term advancement.

## The Bottom Line

Some might still ask: why is this responsibility so heavily placed on men? The answer is practical, not ideological: men currently hold the vast majority of CEO and senior leadership roles in tech, meaning they control the levers of power required for systemic change. True leadership excellence means intentionally using that majority platform to pull women into high-visibility roles and decision-making circles.

The financial return on this leadership shift is clear: McKinsey & Company data show that diverse teams are [25% more likely to outperform their peers](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/featured%20insights/diversity%20and%20inclusion/diversity%20wins%20how%20inclusion%20matters/diversity-wins-how-inclusion-matters-vf.pdf) financially.

Building a high-performance culture requires the courage to dismantle toxic, legacy mindsets, even when it feels uncomfortable or goes unsupported by peers. But true leadership is rarely comfortable. It's about driving bottom-line business value and building a legacy. And ultimately, it's about creating change that matters universally. 

We cannot fix a leaky bucket without the people who built the plumbing. It is time for male allies to use their leverage, optimise the environment, and turn the tech sector into an industry where top talent doesn't just enter, but stays.
